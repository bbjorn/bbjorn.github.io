Educator

Both as a designer and as an educator my interest have lied in the interaction between humans and computers. As a UX designer I learned how to improve people’s experience with applications, while as an educator I am teaching students about technology. As I gained more experience in both fields it became clear to me that the soft skills that make great designers are the same as those that make great teachers.

For example being able to articulate critique of a product in such a way that you can explain both the good and the bad parts of it is a skill that all designers should have. In the same way a great teacher will tell a student both what they did well and what they need to improve in an assignment they have done. Whereas an average designer or teacher might zero in on either the good or the bad parts of the feedback, or worse not give any at all.

In my experience, another skill that great designers and educators have in common is being able to communicate in a manner that makes people listen to them. You might be pitching the idea of the century or teaching the most interesting subject ever, but if you can’t grab people's attention they are going to forget what you were talking about a few minutes after they have left the room.

The last skill I want to highlight is to be a good listener. Being able to listen to and understand someone is crucial while doing user research, during a discussion with the team or in a classroom, or when a student or tester is explaining why they got stuck on a task. It can help you notice ideas that might otherwise have been drowned in noise, or give someone the confidence to tell you a problem that they are feeling embarrassed about.

I believe that both educators and designers have a lot of things they can learn from each other, and being good at one of them will give you a leg up on the other.

Developer

The image below is the result of a project I did in an advanced computer graphics course. It's a procedural generation, based upon a ray marching algorithm. The code can be found here, where it has been uploaded to a website called Shadertoy, which not only hosts but also renders it live. Even though it is a few years old now I still consider it the coolest thing I have ever programmed.

Since then my coding has mostly been done in service of something else. I have created simple robots in Arduino to show that a concept can be turned into a real physical project. I have created various websites mostly because I wanted to test different languages and frameworks for front-end development. And lately I have mostly been creating programs that are the solutions to various assignments I have been giving out.

I try to teach programming in a classroom environment in an iterative manner that is close to the way that I am approaching a programming problem. For example an assignment might start with asking a student to read a name from the console. The next step could be to ask the students to add a “Hello” infront of the name before printing it. Next step could be to change the capitalization to the name so that it always has proper capitalization. Keep adding small challenges that increase in difficulty until the program has turned from something that reads names to cypher that encodes and decodes secret messages.

The small steps makes the final problem seem less daunting for beginner students, while more advanced students get to the harder problem quicker. The same way I would try to deconstruct a problem before starting coding.

Lastly, I want to mention how beneficial it is to both have knowledge about UX and coding, regardless of which role I have. To begin with it makes communication easier, as knowledge of the terms and tools used by both professions. As a designer it makes me more aware of difficulties involved in implementing different designs, which would mean that less time and money is wasted on exploring a concept that ultimately will have to be scrapped. And as a developer I have a greater understanding on how different implementations might be seen by the end-user.
